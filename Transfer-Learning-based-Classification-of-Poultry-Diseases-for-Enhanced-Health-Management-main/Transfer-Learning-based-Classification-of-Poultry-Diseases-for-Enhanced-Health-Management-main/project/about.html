Poultry diseases

Poultry diseases can be caused by viruses, bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and parasites. Some of the most prevalent and impactful ones include:

viral diseases

Avian Influenza (AI) / Bird Flu: Can be low or highly pathogenic. Causes respiratory distress, listlessness, loss of appetite, and can lead to high mortality, especially with highly pathogenic strains.

Marek's Disease: Caused by a herpesvirus, leading to tumors in various organs and paralysis. No treatment, but preventable by vaccination. Infectious Bronchitis (IB): Rapidly spreading respiratory disease, especially in young chicks, affecting egg production and shell quality in layers. Some strains also cause kidney disease.

Fowlpox: Causes crusty, nodular lesions on unfeathered skin ("dry form") or lesions in the mouth and trachea causing suffocation ("wet form").

Infectious Bursal Disease (IBD) / Gumboro Disease: Affects the immune system, primarily in young chickens, making them susceptible to other infections. behavior

Infectious Laryngotracheitis (ILT): A severe respiratory disease causing difficulty breathing, coughing, and blood-tinged mucus in the trachea.

Avian Encephalomyelitis (AE): Affects the central nervous system, particularly in chicks 1-3 weeks old, causing tremors and paralysis.

Fowl Cholera: Caused by Pasteurella multocida. Can be acute (sudden death) or chronic (swollen joints, wattles, respiratory signs).

Salmonellosis (Pullorum Disease, Fowl Typhoid, Paratyphoid): Can cause high mortality in young chicks (Pullorum/Typhoid) or be carried by older birds. Can also be a food safety concern (e.g., Salmonella enteritidis).

Colibacillosis: Caused by E. coli, often a secondary infection leading to various conditions like peritonitis, salpingitis, and respiratory issues.

Mycoplasmosis (Chronic Respiratory Disease - CRD): Caused by Mycoplasma gallisepticum or Mycoplasma synoviae. Leads to respiratory signs (coughing, sneezing, nasal discharge), swollen joints, and decreased production.

Necrotic Enteritis: Caused by Clostridium perfringens, leading to severe intestinal damage and rapid mortality in fast-growing birds.

Omphalitis (Navel III): Bacterial infection of the navel in newly hatched chicks, often due to poor hygiene during hatching. Parasitic Diseases: Coccidiosis: Caused by protozoa (coccidia) that multiply in the intestinal lining, leading to diarrhea, poor growth, and mortality. Internal Parasites (Worms): Roundworms, cecal worms, tapeworms can cause poor growth, reduced egg production, and general unthriftiness, especially in birds with outdoor access. External Parasites (Lice, Mites): Cause irritation, feather damage, anemia (in severe cases), and stress, which can weaken immunity.
